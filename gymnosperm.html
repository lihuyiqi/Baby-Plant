Gymnosperm

Evolution

As the sister clade of angiosperms, gymnosperms are seed plants that have much less studies and information on their evolution. This is due to their long period of evolution and many extinctions of various species that create huge gaps among the four remaining morphologically distinct extant groups.[1] Nowadays, with only 1000 living species,[2] gymnosperms represent four of the five seed plant lineages: conifers (Pinophyta), cycads (Cycadophyta), ginkgos (Ginkgophyta), and gnetophytes (Gnetophyta).[3] The origin of seed plants can be traced back to the Devonian period, which was about 400 million years ago. They once attained proliferation during the Mesozoic period (250 million years ago), where a warmer and wetter environment favored their domination on land before their decline in the late Cretaceous period.[1] During this time, extreme climate change resulted in major extinctions of gymnosperms and diversification of angiosperms. From seed ferns that rely on water as pathway of reproduction, gymnosperms went through multiple adaptations such as seeds, pollen, and wood to survive in drier environments.[4] Moreover, compared to the South hemisphere, the North hemisphere has a higher species turnover rate since older lineages were replaced by those better adapted to cooler and drier environmental conditions.[2]

Water physiology

Different to angiosperms whose cell walls are constituted of xylem vessels and tracheids, gymnosperms except gnetophytes are made solely of tracheids, whose dual function is water transport and mechanical support.[3] This absence of vessels in gymnosperms leads to a simpler plant structure. In habitats with low soil moisture availability, gymnosperms can improve their hydraulic efficiency by increasing pit membrane diameter and avoid embolism by increasing their wood density and expanding their tracheid diameter.[5] In addition, one important component of the plant cell wall is cellulose; it controls mechanical properties of the cell and enhances plants’ adaptation ability to the environment. Cellulose is biosynthesized by cellulose synthases (CesA), and research has shown that gymnosperms possess a less complex CesA genes’ regulation network than angiosperms. This mechanism is also affected by the quantity of lignin, another component in the plant cell wall, in gymnosperms.[3]

Reproductive Mechanisms

Originally, the first form of plants was ferns and mosses. These ancestors required the presence of water to reproduce since the male gametophyte releases flagellated sperm, which must swim to reach and fertilize the female gamete. The fertilized zygote will later develop into a diploid sporophyte and produce haploid spores once matured. Ferns and mosses are mostly homosporous; they only produce one type of spore, and their gametophytes mature independently from spores. During their proliferation, which was the Mississippian and Pennsylvanian periods, the climate was mostly wet. However, the following Permian period was dry, and the climate changed forced the seedless vascular plants to evolve the mechanism of seeds. Seed plants are heterosporous, producing megaspores (female) and microspores (male). The size of their gametophyte reduced to a microscopic cluster of cells enclosed in the tissues of the sporophyte. Moreover, the adaptation of pollen also facilitated seed plant’s independence on water for gamete fertilization and development of the embryo. Pollen grains are male gametophytes which can travel long distance with wind-pollination and which have a protective coat that prevents desiccation and mechanical damage.[4] On the other hand, a haploid nourishing tissue surrounds the diploid embryo in the seed to protect it, to furnish necessary amounts of nutrients,[3] and to maintain dormancy for tens or thousands of years, until the growth conditions are optimal.[4] Therefore, the major difference between gymnosperms and angiosperms is the enclosure of ovules and seeds; gymnosperms are characterized with naked or partially naked ovules whereas angiosperms have them enclosed in an ovary.[1]

Despite its rarity, dioecy is found in almost 65% of the gymnosperm species, which means the majority of gymnosperms have individual male and female plants.[6] They predominantly rely on anemophily (wind pollination) and wind-mediated seed dispersal for cross-pollination. However, it is suggested that some of the ancient gymnosperm species were insect-pollinated, just as angiosperms, but they became extinct with their pollinators.[3] The separation between male and female plants ensures the total exclusion of the risk of self-pollination and the optimization of resource allocation to both female and male functions. Nevertheless, this characteristic presents numerous disadvantages such as limited opportunities of mating if no other partner is near and reduced seed dispersal since seeds are only carried by female, which represents half of the plant population.[6] Their high level of within-population genetic diversity combined with mating disadvantages can explain their high species turnover rate and the possible lead to extinction of some species.[3]

Conifers

Conifers represent two-thirds of living gymnosperms and constitute 39% of world forests.[2] With the evolution of seed and pollen, gymnosperms are adapted to live in cold and dry weather, where water is scarce. The narrow shape of their leaves and a thick cuticle prevent water evaporation and explain the phylum’s predominance at high attitudes. While most gymnosperms are dioecious, conifers are mostly monoecious.[4] Their male and female reproductive structures are unisexual and occupy different locations on a same tree. The female structures are at the top of the tree while male structures develop at the base.[3] The sporophylls are developed in different size of strobili, often known as cones, where male cones are smaller than female’s.[4] The far and distinct placements of their reproductive structures also limit the self-pollination in conifers and ensures the genetic diversity of this dominant phylum of gymnosperms.[3]

Ginkgo

The phylum of ginkgophytes is represented by the single surviving species, Ginkgo biloba, which also has the name of “living fossil”. The Ginkgoales first appeared during the lower Jurassic.[4] However, their inefficient physiological plasticity, which is their ability to alter phenotypes and plant structure to a higher fitness level than the original one in extreme conditions,[7] resulted in their extinction during the Triassic-Jurassic mass extinction period. This period is known for massive ecological changes, where the [O2]: [CO2] in the environment significantly decreased.[8] Moreover, one interesting characteristic of ginkgo is its herbivore-free defense mechanism. Ginkgo foliage produces ginkgolides, a potent antifeeding defense. It stops pests from feeding on the tree rather than killing them. Nowadays, it can adapt to environmental pests and pathogen pressure by changing the effectiveness of their defense behavior.[3]
