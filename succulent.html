Succulent

While resurrection plants are drought-tolerant, succulent plants use a different survival strategy called drought avoidance. Instead of evolving to survive the loss of cellular water, succulents have evolved physiologies centered around high capacitance, which is the ability to store large amounts of water in their tissues to prevent their internal system from ever reaching a dehydrated state. Some of the important physiologies that allow the survival of succulents in dry environments are:

    Elastic water storage cells: Succulent organs contain large volumes of specialized storage cells. These cells have a low modulus of elasticity, meaning that their cell walls are highly flexible. This not only allows a large volume for water storage but also allows the plant to release their stored water to their photosynthetic tissues without a large change in its internal water potential. A high water potential would cause rapid movement of water out of the cells, resulting in dehydration in the cells.
    Ribbed stem architecture: In genera like Ferocactus or Carnigiea, the stem surface is not smooth but convoluted with ribs. These ribs allow the plant to shrink or swell like an accordion as it gains or loses water volume. This prevents the epidermis from rupturing during massive changes in diameter due to different seasonal conditions for water availability.
    Wide-band tracheids: Succulents have specialized water conducting cells in their xylem with spirals or ring-like thickenings on their cell walls. These structures provide support to resist conduit collapse when the surrounding tissues contract during drought but also allow flexibility to accommodate the constant change in volume of water.
    Hydraulic isolation: While succulents have high capacitance, they must also prevent the stored water from being pulled back into the dry soil.
One of the strategies succulents adopt is controlled cavitation where some xylems are intentionally filled with air to avoid the backflow of water from plant to soil. Another strategy is suberization of root cells where the root cells are reduced in water permeability and therefore prevent water backflow.

Because of their high water storage capacity, succulents do not need to be watered as much compared to most of the other plants in the category of angiosperm.
Eamples of plants: Aloe, Jade plant, Agave

Reference: Schulte PJ. Water transport processes in desert succulent plants. In: de la Barrera E, Smith WK, editors. Perspectives in Biophysical Plant Ecophysiology. Mexico: Universidad Nacional Autónoma de México; 2009. p. 39–55.
